Sertraline ✕ Birth Control
Critical
SSRIs can reduce hormonal contraceptive efficacy and amplify cycle disruption when combined.
🔴 May reduce contraceptive reliability · Worsens mood swings in luteal phase · Can shift period by 3–7 days

Mechanism: Sertraline affects CYP3A4 enzyme activity, which is responsible for metabolizing many synthetic estrogens and progestins. This may reduce plasma levels of contraceptive hormones by up to 30–40%, potentially compromising efficacy. Additionally, combined serotonin + hormone fluctuations worsen premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) symptoms.

Recommendation: Discuss with prescriber. Consider non-hormonal contraception alternatives or confirm dosage timing.

Ibuprofen ✕ Luteal Phase
Moderate
NSAID use during the luteal phase may inhibit prostaglandins needed for normal menstruation onset.
🟡 May delay period by 1–3 days · Reduces ovulation marker visibility · Can mask cramp pain that signals issues

Mechanism: Ibuprofen inhibits COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes, suppressing prostaglandin synthesis. In the luteal phase, prostaglandins play a key role in triggering the uterine contractions that initiate menstruation. Chronic use (3+ days) can therefore delay or lighten periods.

Recommendation: Use sparingly (1–2 days max). Paracetamol is a safer alternative for cycle-related pain management.

Metformin ✕ Iron (Diet)
Info
Metformin can reduce B12 and iron absorption, which is especially relevant during and after menstruation.
🟢 Monitor iron levels · Consider iron-rich foods post-period · B12 supplementation may be advised

Detail: Long-term Metformin use is associated with reduced absorption of Vitamin B12 (by up to 30%) and can indirectly affect iron metabolism. During menstruation, iron losses are already elevated — this combination may increase risk of mild anaemia.

Action: Ask your doctor about periodic B12 monitoring. Emphasise iron-rich foods (spinach, lentils, red meat) in the days following your period.
